There's a particular feeling in a room when something has genuinely shifted.

Last Tuesday, 40 or so Longmont-area builders, business owners, and serious AI practitioners showed up at the meetup and watched 14 slides that all pointed in the same direction: the demo phase is over.

Not the "AI is overhyped" direction. The other one. The one where the tools actually work and the question is no longer *if* but *which one, for what, at what cost, and who verifies it.*

That's the meetup. Here's what landed.

---

## The Biggest Thing Nobody Was Talking About Six Months Ago

Agent marketplaces are real.

Slide 4 showed numbers that landed hard. One platform, AgentHansa: 93,984 agents, 241 active merchants, $35,778 earned by operators in the most recent reporting window. These aren't toy agents. They're fulfilling quests, delivering outputs, getting rated.

The economic layer is forming. Someone builds the agent. Someone else posts a task. A price gets quoted. The agent executes. A human verifies. Payment clears.

This is not a thought experiment. It's a market with a clearing price.

For local builders: you're either building agents, buying agent labor, or routing between both. The people in that room were mostly already doing at least one of those three things.

---

## Frontier Models Are a Routing Problem Now

The benchmark tables are a mess. Gemini, Claude, GPT-5 — winning on different tasks, different cost structures, different latency profiles. The idea that you pick one frontier model and commit is already outdated. What the best builders are doing is routing: sending coding tasks to one model, analysis to another, creative work to a third, based on price-performance at the task level.

This is not exotic. It's choosing a different vendor for different parts of your stack. The new discipline is treating AI model selection the same way — as a routing decision, not a religious commitment.

For a business owner: stop paying GPT-5 prices for tasks that Gemini Flash handles at a tenth of the cost. Route accordingly.

---

## The Token Meter Is Running

When tokens are cheap in a demo, you don't think about them. When you're running 10,000 agent loops a day and each one burns 50 cents in context and inference, token accounting becomes a first-class business concern.

This is the discipline that's emerging: treating AI compute cost the way you treat cloud infrastructure costs. Set budgets. Track cost per task type. Optimize the expensive paths. Route cheap tasks to cheap models.

The builders who figure this out first have a permanent cost advantage. The ones who ignore it will have a nasty billing surprise in six months.

---

## Coding Agents Stopped Being Demos

不再是 "look what the agent built in 30 seconds." It's agents running for hours, managing dependencies, handling multistep refactors, executing against long-running coding goals with checkpoint verification built in.

Benchmark data from the deck showed agentic coding performance on SWE-Bench, MCP Atlas (multi-step workflows), and Toolathlon (real-world tool use) all moving meaningfully upward quarter over quarter.

The takeaway: if you have a dev team, they're already using these tools or they will be soon. If you're a non-technical founder, you're being outrun by competitors who have agents doing the work that used to require headcount.

---

## Specs Are the New Product Surface

The analogy that worked for the whole evening: in software, the spec has always been the real product. The code is just the implementation. Now AI is making that literal — because when you can route work to agents, the spec becomes the interface.

Write the spec well, and the agents execute it. Write it poorly, and they execute that with equal fidelity.

This is why prompt engineering is becoming product specification. The people getting real leverage from AI are the ones who write tight, unambiguous specs. Not poetry. Not elaborate system prompts. Clear inputs, clear outputs, clear constraints.

---

## The Punchline Nobody Wanted to Say Out Loud

The human job moves upstream.

Not disappears. Moves. Upstream.

The work that AI takes over is the repetitive, the measurable, the verifiable. What remains — and what becomes more valuable — is choosing the right problem, designing the loop, pricing the work, and verifying the output.

That's not a comforting abstraction. It's a specific job description. And it's the job that the people in that room were already doing, or need to start doing deliberately.
