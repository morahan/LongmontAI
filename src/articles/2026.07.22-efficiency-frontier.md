## The Efficiency Frontier

The July release cycle did not produce one uncontested winner. It produced a
better question: for a real task, which model and harness deliver the required
quality with the least money, time, and operational risk?

That question is the **efficiency frontier**. A configuration is on the frontier
when no other available configuration is at least as good on the outcomes that
matter while being strictly better on one of them. In practice, the outcomes are
task quality, total cost, latency, reliability, access, and the harness that
actually ran the work. A leaderboard point by itself is not a frontier.

The slides lead with the claim, then the evidence: new releases are narrowing the
gap between raw capability and economical execution. For Longmont builders, that
means route by measured task outcome, not by the loudest benchmark headline.

## Four releases redraw the map

**Grok 4.5 arrived on July 8 as SpaceXAI's coding, agentic-work, and knowledge-
work flagship.** Its release post reports 64.7% on SWE-Bench Pro, 29.0% on SWE
Marathon, 80 tokens per second, and $2 / $6 per million input/output tokens. The
important detail is not the marketing superlative; it is the claim that the model
uses materially fewer output tokens on the same class of engineering task. That
is an efficiency claim that deserves a local eval, not blind trust.

**Meta followed on July 9 with Muse Spark 1.1.** The update adds a public preview
of the Meta Model API and emphasizes multimodal reasoning, coding, computer use,
tool use, and orchestration across parallel subagents. It is a strong reminder
that an agent model is not just an answer generator: it is increasingly a
coordinator that must keep state and act safely across tools.

**Claude Fable 5 is back.** Anthropic redeployed Fable 5 on July 1 after the US
government lifted the export controls that had forced a global suspension. The
model is again available across Claude's first-party surfaces, with cloud-provider
availability returning separately. The access interruption is part of the
benchmark result: a top score has less operational value when a workflow cannot
reliably reach the model.

**GPT-5.6 became generally available on July 9.** OpenAI's family makes the
product tiers unusually explicit: **Sol** is the flagship, **Terra** is the
everyday balance of capability and cost, and **Luna** is the fast, cost-efficient
option. Their posted API prices are $5 / $30, $2.50 / $15, and $1 / $6 per
million input/output tokens respectively. That is the practical shape of a model
router: one difficult task may earn Sol; routine work may belong on Terra or
Luna; the choice should be validated with the same harness and acceptance test.

## What the charts do, and do not, say

![Artificial Analysis comparison of intelligence and estimated cost per task, supplied as a July 2026 snapshot.](/weekly-screenshots/2026.07.22/artificial-analysis-intelligence-cost-frontier.png)

The highlighted upper-left region is the visual intuition behind an efficiency
frontier: high performance at lower cost. But it is only a snapshot of a specific
index, estimated task cost, and listed configurations. A model can move when the
reasoning setting changes, prompts get longer, tool calls fail, or the provider
changes access. Use a chart to create a shortlist, then test the shortlist on
your own work.

![CursorBench snapshot comparing model score and average cost per task.](/weekly-screenshots/2026.07.22/cursorbench-model-cost-score.png)

The CursorBench view makes the same tradeoff more concrete: the line is not a
universal ranking. It is a set of observed configurations. The right question is
not "which dot is highest?" It is "which available dot clears our quality bar at
the cheapest acceptable total cost and turnaround time?"

## DeepSWE is useful because it keeps the task hard

![DeepSWE v1.1 leaderboard graphic supplied for this edition.](/weekly-screenshots/2026.07.22/deepswe-v1.1.svg)

DeepSWE v1.1 is a strong reality check because it evaluates original, long-
horizon engineering tasks and runs models on a common mini-swe-agent harness.
Its July 13 leaderboard places GPT-5.6 Sol at 73% +/- 3%, Fable 5 at 70% +/- 4%,
Terra at 70% +/- 3%, and Luna at 67% +/- 4%. The adjacent confidence intervals,
costs, token counts, and agent steps are the story. The frontier is a cloud of
tradeoffs, not a podium.

The value of a shared harness is comparability. The limit is just as important:
production prompts, repositories, permissions, retries, and review gates can
change the answer. Re-run the decision on your own acceptance set before turning
an external leaderboard into a production default.

## A caution on AI and assessment claims

![Cropped social-media claim about take-home and in-person economics exam scores. It is presented as an unverified claim, not evidence that students cheated.](/weekly-screenshots/2026.07.22/paul-graham-ai-cheating-claim.png)

![Cropped economics exam score comparison accompanying the social-media claim.](/weekly-screenshots/2026.07.22/econ-1170-midterm-final-score-chart.png)

The supplied post makes a serious claim from a single score distribution. The
chart may be worth discussing, but it cannot establish that "all but three"
students cheated. Cohort change, withdrawals, assessment design, grading,
preparation, and selection effects are all plausible confounders. The more useful
lesson for educators is methodological: design assessments that make reasoning
visible, explain what evidence would change the conclusion, and avoid treating a
viral chart as a causal study.

## Real-time video is moving up a resolution tier

{{video:/weekly-screenshots/2026.07.22/wan-streamer-v0.2-demo.mp4}}

[Wan-Streamer v0.2: Higher Resolution, Same Latency](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2607.04443)
reports a native-streaming audio-visual model that increases its interactive
stream from 192 x 336 to 640 x 368 while retaining roughly 200 ms of model-side
signal-to-signal latency at 25 FPS. The authors describe a split between a
low-latency single-GPU "thinker" path and a multi-GPU context-parallel
"performer" for the expensive video generation work.

This is a useful counterpoint to the model-release race. For a real-time agent,
resolution, motion, speech, responsiveness, and scene grounding are coupled
system constraints. The demo is impressive precisely because it asks whether the
experience stays legible while the latency budget stays human.

## What to do this week

- **Build a small local frontier eval.** Pick five to ten representative tasks,
  define an acceptance test, and record quality, wall-clock time, tokens, tool
  failures, and total cost.
- **Test the whole system.** Hold the harness fixed when comparing models. Then
  compare harnesses separately. Otherwise a model result may really be a workflow
  result.
- **Route by threshold.** Give the most capable tier the tasks that need it;
  promote routine work only when the cheaper tier fails the acceptance test.
- **Treat access as an engineering variable.** Keep an approved fallback for
  critical workflows, especially when model access, regions, and plans can change.
- **Keep evidence calibrated.** Vendor benchmarks, independent benchmarks, social
  screenshots, and research papers have different evidentiary weight. Label them
  accordingly.
